Ideological Empires:
The Resource of Belief

Industrialisation created unprecedented new resources. But it also created an unprecedented question: who should control them?

The Big Idea

Not all resources are physical

Over the past few weeks, you've traced how resources shape turning points ‚Äî coal powering the Industrial Revolution, food determining who survives a famine, infrastructure shaping who recovers from a disaster. But there's a category of resource you may not have considered yet.

Beliefs are resources too. They determine who is allowed to take more, who is expected to sacrifice, which futures are considered acceptable, and what counts as "common sense."

The connection to last week: Industrialisation created new physical resources ‚Äî coal, factory labour, capital, urban land. The debate between capitalism and communism emerged as two competing answers to the question: who should control these new resources?

Two Systems

Capitalism vs. Communism

Both systems emerged as responses to industrialisation. Both claim to manage resources better. Click each figure to learn more.

Capitalism
Market Economy
TAP TO EXPLORE ‚Üí

Market Economy

Core idea: Resources are controlled by private individuals and groups. Competition drives efficiency and innovation.
Goal: Economic growth through competition and profit.
Examples: USA, Singapore, most of Western Europe.
Often creates innovation and wealth ‚Äî but can deepen inequality. Who benefits when resources are allocated by the market?
Communism
Command Economy
TAP TO EXPLORE ‚Üí

Command Economy

Core idea: Resources are controlled by the state on behalf of the people. Central planning replaces competition.
Goal: Equality and planned development for all.
Examples: USSR (historical), North Korea, Cuba.
Can protect the vulnerable and reduce inequality ‚Äî but often limits individual freedom. Who decides what "the people" need?

üí° Key Insight

These aren't just economic systems ‚Äî they're belief systems about who deserves what. Every policy, every budget, every trade deal reflects an underlying belief about how resources should flow. The question isn't just "which system works better?" ‚Äî it's "better for whom?"

The Debate

Centralized control or individual freedom?

Debate Question

"Which system better manages resources for long-term societal stability: centralized control or individual freedom?"

Debate preparation

You will be assigned a side. Use your PCQ work and the content from this site to build your case.

1

Prepare 3 points with evidence

Each point should connect a belief to a resource outcome. Use the country examples, your PCQ perspectives, or historical evidence from the Industrial Revolution.

2

Anticipate 2 points from the other side

Your PCQ work should help here ‚Äî if you did a perspective from "the other side," you already know their strongest points.

3

Think about how to refute them

Don't just disagree ‚Äî show why your system handles that specific challenge better. Use evidence, not just opinion.

Connecting to your summative

The debate practices a skill you'll need for your summative short story: making a convincing argument through narrative. The strongest debaters don't just state facts ‚Äî they tell a story about how their system works.

Story Framing

Turn your argument into a story

Take the debate side you found most compelling and frame it into a narrative. This practices the same skill you'll use for your summative.

Required elements (minimum 6 parts)

IntroDefine the ideology and its core belief about resources.
Point 1: Resource ManagementExplain how this belief system manages resources ‚Äî which ones, and how?
Point 2: Example in ActionGive a historical or contemporary example of this belief shaping a real turning point.
Point 3: Future PredictionPredict a future outcome if this belief continues to guide resource decisions.
CounterargumentPresent the opposing ideology's view. Can you refute it?
ConclusionArgue why your chosen belief system leads to a better societal outcome.

Choose your challenge level

LEVEL A

Restate and persuade

Restate your claim clearly. Add rhetorical or narrative flair ‚Äî use emotional or persuasive language to engage your audience.

LEVEL B

Build a story

Turn your claim into a story. Make sure to explicitly connect key moments to big themes like control, freedom, fairness, or stability.

LEVEL C

Analyse an existing story

Use an existing real-world story (company ad, government policy, activist report). Analyse how it reflects your chosen belief system. Focus on how the story works ‚Äî not just what it says.

The key insight: This ad never says "capitalism" or "individual freedom." But it's making a powerful argument about who deserves access to resources like opportunity, recognition, and public space. That's what makes it a Level C example ‚Äî the ideology is embedded in the story, not stated directly.

The 6 story elements, applied:

1
Core Belief / Ideology

Individual perseverance and self-determination define success. Nobody has the right to limit your potential based on their discomfort. Personal power matters more than societal gatekeeping.

2
How This Belief Manages Resources (Point 1)

The "resource" here is opportunity and self-expression. In this belief system, opportunity should be earned through effort, not limited by social norms ‚Äî discomfort with difference, disability, or perceived ability shouldn't determine who gets to participate.

3
Example in Action (Point 2)

The ad itself is the example. The runner's success story would traditionally be dismissed by a society that defines athleticism narrowly (able-bodied, elite). The ad flips this: "Your discomfort with my existence doesn't erase my right to be here."

4
Future Prediction (Point 3)

If this belief continues: more inclusive spaces for athletes of all backgrounds, abilities, and identities. Societies will value effort and passion over gatekeeping and rigid definitions of who "belongs."

5
Counterargument

Opposing belief: Achievement belongs to those who meet certain standards. Society defines who counts as "real" competitors. "We're just trying to maintain the level of the sport." (Note the underlying exclusionary tone.)

6
Conclusion

Inclusive perseverance ideologies create societies where success stories multiply ‚Äî not just for the privileged, but for anyone willing to show up and try. Limiting who can participate limits what a society can achieve. "Just Do It" becomes not just personal, but political: a claim that effort itself is worthy, no matter the gatekeepers.

üí° Notice what this example does

It takes a commercial ‚Äî something you see every day ‚Äî and reads it as an ideological argument about resources. That's what Level C asks you to do: find a real-world story and analyse how it reflects a belief system. Level B does something similar, but you create the story rather than finding one.
